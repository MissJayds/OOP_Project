Bitwise Operators: Explanation and Example

What are Bitwise Operators?

Bitwise operators are used to perform bit-level operations on integers. They work on the binary representation of numbers and operate directly on their individual bits. The most commonly used bitwise operators are:

List of Bitwise Operators

Operator Description Example
& Bitwise AND 5 & 3 = 1
| Bitwise OR 5 | 3 = 7
^ Bitwise XOR 5 ^ 3 = 6
~ Bitwise NOT ~5 = -6
<< Left Shift 5 << 1 = 10
>> Right Shift 5 >> 1 = 2

Detailed Explanation

Here are some more details on each of the bitwise operators:

  • AND (&): The result of bitwise AND is 1 if both bits are 1, otherwise the result is 0.
  • OR (|): The result of bitwise OR is 1 if at least one of the bits is 1.
  • XOR (^): The result of bitwise XOR is 1 if only one of the bits is 1, otherwise the result is 0.
  • NOT (~): Bitwise NOT inverts all the bits. This is a unary operator.
  • Left Shift (<<): The bits of the number are shifted to the left by the specified number of positions. This operation is equivalent to multiplying the number by 2 for each shift.
  • Right Shift (>>): The bits of the number are shifted to the right by the specified number of positions. This operation is equivalent to dividing the number by 2 for each shift.

Example

Let's take two integers: 5 and 3, whose binary forms are:

5 = 101 (in binary)

3 = 011 (in binary)

Now, applying the AND operator:

5 & 3 = 101 & 011 = 001 (binary) = 1 (decimal)

So, 5 & 3 = 1.
